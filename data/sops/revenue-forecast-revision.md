# Forecast Revision Workflow

**Property:** Juniper Creek Ranch, Fredericksburg, TX
**Department:** Revenue Management
**Document Owner:** General Manager
**Version:** 1.0

## Purpose

The forecast is the property's best estimate of future occupancy, rate, and revenue. It drives labor scheduling, F&B ordering, purchasing, and cash-flow planning. A stale or unrevised forecast compounds error across every operational function. This procedure defines how the forecast at Juniper Creek Ranch is built, revised, and communicated to the team, and how the forecast connects to the daily yield and rate decisions.

## Scope

Applies to the General Manager (holding the Revenue Manager function). Covers the 90-day rolling forecast, quarterly budget forecasts, and annual planning forecast. Does not cover month-end financial close reporting, which is an Accounting function.

## Responsibilities

- **General Manager:** Owns the forecast; performs revisions per the cadence below; communicates revisions to the team.
- **Director of Housekeeping:** Uses the forecast for labor planning; provides input on deep-clean rotation capacity affecting forecast.
- **F&B Supervisor:** Uses the forecast for purchasing and F&B labor; provides input on known private events affecting covers.
- **Front Office:** Uses the forecast for staffing and group arrival preparation.
- **Ownership:** Reviews monthly forecast revisions as part of ownership operations check-in.

## Forecast Components

The forecast at Juniper Creek Ranch is built from the following inputs.

### Occupancy Forecast

- Current on-the-books (reservations confirmed in Maestro).
- Group pickup projections from active group blocks.
- Pace projections based on historical booking patterns.
- Market demand signals (local events, regional trends).

### Rate Forecast

- Current BAR by date.
- Group rate mix.
- Forecast channel mix (direct vs. OTA vs. group) which affects effective rate.
- Any planned rate adjustments.

### Revenue Forecast

- Room revenue = Occupancy × Rate.
- F&B revenue projected based on occupancy and historical capture rates.
- Other revenue (spa, events, add-ons) based on historical and booked activity.

## Revision Cadence

The forecast is revised at defined intervals.

| Forecast | Revised | Published | Distributed To |
|---|---|---|---|
| 14-Day Tactical | Daily | Same-day | Director of Housekeeping, F&B Supervisor |
| 30-Day Operational | Weekly (Monday) | Monday afternoon | Full management team |
| 90-Day Rolling | Monthly (1st of month) | Within 3 business days | Ownership, full management team |
| Quarterly Budget Check | Quarterly (Q-end + 5 business days) | Within 7 business days | Ownership |
| Annual Plan | Annually (October for following year) | By November 15 | Ownership |

## The 14-Day Tactical Forecast

This is the forecast the operating team uses for labor scheduling, ordering, and daily decision-making.

### Daily Build Sequence

1. At the start of each morning's rate review (see Daily Rate Review SOP), pull the 14-day pace report from Maestro.
2. Compare on-the-books to the same period's forecast from 7 days ago. Is the forecast trending accurate?
3. Factor in any new information since yesterday:
   - New group signed (adds definite pickup to the forecast dates).
   - Group cancellation (removes pickup).
   - Weather forecast change affecting demand.
   - News event (local disaster, major event cancellation) affecting demand.
4. Adjust the 14-day forecast in the Forecast Tracker spreadsheet.
5. Publish the updated forecast to the Director of Housekeeping and F&B Supervisor by email by noon.

### What "Publishing" Means

The forecast email is not optional reading. It includes:

- The 14 days out by date, by occupancy forecast, by forecast F&B covers.
- Any revision from yesterday's forecast and the reason.
- Any known group activity, VIP arrival, or special event in the window.
- Labor and purchasing implications if significant (e.g., "Saturday now forecasts 92% vs. 78% yesterday — Housekeeping plan needs +1 RA, F&B prep +20% on breakfast").

### Housekeeping Use

The Director of Housekeeping uses the 14-day forecast to:

1. Build the weekly RA schedule.
2. Order linen and amenity supply.
3. Plan the deep-clean rotation around low-occupancy days.

### F&B Use

The F&B Supervisor uses the 14-day forecast to:

1. Build the Server and Bartender schedule.
2. Adjust food and beverage orders.
3. Plan any special menu or feature for known high-occupancy days.

## The 30-Day Operational Forecast

Every Monday afternoon, the GM produces the 30-day operational forecast for the full management team.

### Build Sequence

1. Pull the 30-day pace report from Maestro.
2. Update each day's forecast based on current on-the-books, confirmed groups, and pace assumptions.
3. Build the forecast by room type and by segment (transient, group, negotiated).
4. Review the forecast against the same period's budget. Is the property on pace, ahead, or behind?
5. Identify any date where forecast materially diverges from expected (for example, a weekend suddenly projecting low occupancy due to cancelled group).
6. Note required interventions (promotion, rate adjustment, outbound sales push).

### Distribution

1. Email the 30-day forecast to the full management team by Monday 4:00 PM.
2. Include a 1-paragraph summary: "Week of X looks Y; note that Z."
3. The Tuesday morning management huddle opens with a 10-minute review of the forecast. Questions and implications are discussed there.

## The 90-Day Rolling Forecast

On the first business day of each month, the GM produces the 90-day rolling forecast.

### Build Sequence

1. Pull the 90-day pace report from Maestro.
2. Pull the 90-day group pickup report.
3. Pull the 90-day event calendar for the region (local festivals, wine country events, weddings expected).
4. Build the forecast by month, by week, by occupancy, rate, and revenue.
5. Compare to the budget for the same period. Document variance and cause.
6. Review group block exposure — how much of the 90-day revenue is locked in by confirmed groups vs. still needing transient pickup.
7. Identify risk and opportunity:
   - Dates where forecast is well below budget require action.
   - Dates where forecast is well above budget allow for rate compression or restriction tightening.

### Distribution

1. Distributed to ownership and full management team.
2. Reviewed at the monthly ownership operations check-in.
3. Archived in the Forecast folder.

## The Quarterly Budget Check

At each quarter-end plus 5 business days, the GM produces a quarterly forecast-to-actual comparison.

1. Actual revenue by category.
2. Budgeted revenue by category.
3. Variance dollar and percentage.
4. Root cause narrative for any category off budget by more than 5%.
5. Forward implications — does the next quarter's forecast need revision based on what happened this quarter?

Distributed to ownership within 7 business days of quarter-end.

## The Annual Plan

The annual plan is built in October for the following calendar year.

### Key Components

1. Month-by-month occupancy forecast.
2. Month-by-month rate forecast.
3. Month-by-month revenue forecast (rooms, F&B, other).
4. Expense plan (labor, supply, utility, maintenance).
5. Capital plan (major projects, replacements, refurbishments).
6. Key assumptions (market conditions, local events, staffing assumptions).

### Distribution

1. Draft reviewed with ownership in November.
2. Final plan approved by ownership by December 15.
3. Distributed to full management team by January 5 with operational implications.

## Forecast Accuracy Tracking

Every month, the GM compares the forecast from 30 days prior to actual.

1. For each day in the past month, compare forecasted occupancy to actual.
2. Calculate monthly forecast accuracy (absolute error divided by actual).
3. Target: forecast accuracy within 3 percentage points for 14-day forecast; within 5 percentage points for 30-day forecast.
4. Chronic accuracy issues (multiple months outside target) prompt a review of forecast methodology.

## Common Failure Modes

- "Forecasting" by simply copying the budget — the budget is a static target; the forecast is a dynamic estimate.
- Revising the forecast down aggressively when pace is soft, then having to revise back up when late pickup arrives — damages team trust in the forecast.
- Not communicating forecast revisions to Housekeeping and F&B in time to affect labor and purchasing — the forecast exists to drive operational decisions, not to sit in a spreadsheet.
- Ignoring group cancellations in the forecast until the next scheduled revision — forecast revision is event-driven, not just calendar-driven.

## Escalation Triggers

Contact ownership if:
- Monthly forecast is more than 10% below budget with no clear recovery path
- A quarterly forecast-to-actual variance exceeds 10% in any revenue category
- Forecast accuracy consistently falls outside target for 2+ consecutive months
- A major demand disruption (weather event, regional closure, significant market shift) warrants ownership awareness
